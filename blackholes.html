Understanding the Kerr Metric

By now, you should be familiar with the Schwarzschild metric: $$ d\tau^2 = (1 - \frac {2M} r)dt^2 - \frac {dr^2} {(1- \frac {2M} r)} - r^2d\phi $$ If you're saying to yourself "Where's $\theta$?", I say: $\theta$ only complicates things. In order to kill $\theta$, let's just imagine all of our events happening in a single equatorial plane around the gravitating body. The only change in angle is azimuthal, that is, on the equatorial plane.

Provide an image here for azimuthal angle

Also, you should know that the Schwarzschild Metric only describes curved spacetime around non-spinning and uncharged gravitating bodies. Furthermore, in the rest of this page, I will be talking specifically about black holes unless denoted otherwise. These metrics do apply to any other astrophysical body in the universe, but black holes are the main focus here (and also where the most weird things happen).

So now, let's imagine a spinning black hole. Behold, from the heavens, down falls the following metric: $$ d\tau^2 = (1 - \frac {2M} r)dt^2 + \frac {4Ma} r dtd\phi - \frac {dr^2} {1 - \frac {2M} r + \frac {a^2} {r^2}} - (1 + \frac {a^2} {r^2} + \frac {2Ma^2} {r^3})r^2d\phi$$ Falling from the heavens indicates we do not need to derive this metric. However, that does not mean we shouldn't test it to see if it fits with our pre-established rules of general relativity. Our window is narrow, only encompassing the Schwarszchild metric, but can we make the Kerr metric still agree with everything the Schwarzschild metric declares?

Defining the Kerr Metric

The most obvious change from Schwarzschild to Kerr is the introduction of a new variable $a$. $a$ is the angular momentum parameter. The angular momentum parameter describes how "much" the black hole is spinning (in a loose sense). It is defined to be $a = \frac J M$, which is kind of annoying, because we don't know what $J$ is. Well, $J = \frac L m$. Aha! We know what $\frac L m$ is; it's the angular momentum of a particle with mass $m$. I will refresh you on some definitions of angular momentum, though.

Using the Principle of Least Action (or Extremal Aging in Exploring Black Holes), we derived $$ \frac L m = r^2 \frac {d\phi} {d\tau} $$ This means that the angular momentum is defined as the change in angle per wristwatch time, multiplied by the distance from the singularity (squared).

So, the angular momentum parameter $a$ is simply the angular momentum of a black hole per unit mass (of said black hole). There aren't any other new variables, so let's continue with our analysis!

Testing the Kerr Metric

Here is the first and only test of the Kerr metric: when $a = 0$, does it condense to the Schwarszchild metric? Clearly, a non-spinning black hole will no angular momentum, and thus $a = 0$. Let's try it. $$ d\tau^2 = (1 - \frac {2M} r)dt^2 + \frac {4Ma} r dtd\phi - \frac {dr^2} {1 - \frac {2M} r + \frac {a^2} {r^2}} - (1 + \frac {a^2} {r^2} + \frac {2Ma^2} {r^3})r^2d\phi $$ $$ d\tau^2 = (1 - \frac {2M} r)dt^2 + \frac {4M(0)} r dtd\phi - \frac {dr^2} {1 - \frac {2M} r + \frac {(0)^2} {r^2}} - (1 + \frac {(0)^2} {r^2} + \frac {2M(0)^2} {r^3})r^2d\phi $$ $$ d\tau^2 = (1 - \frac {2M} r)dt^2 + 0 - \frac {dr^2} {1 - \frac {2M} r + 0} - (1 + 0 + 0)r^2d\phi $$ $$ d\tau^2 = (1 - \frac {2M} r)dt^2 - \frac {dr^2} {1 - \frac {2M} r} - r^2d\phi $$ Well, it looks right! We've now found that the Kerr metric works for non-spinning black holes, since it successfully whittles down to the Schwarzschild metric.

The next test involves a bit more wordplay and less math. What would happen to a free object that begins to orbit a spinning black hole in the equatorial plane?

It is apparent that the Kerr metric only depends on one static variable $r$. Since it does not depend on static $t$ or static $\phi$, but only $dt$ and $d\phi$ (changes in time or azimuthal angle), we can extrapolate that there are two constants of motion: one involved with $t$ and one involved with $\phi$. With the Schwarzschild metric, we called the constant for $t$ energy ($\frac E m = (1 - \frac {2M} r) \frac {dt} {d\tau}$) and the constant for $\phi$ angular momentum ($\frac L m = r^2 \frac {d\phi} {d\tau}$). Thus if the angular momentum is constant, then the object will not leave the equatorial plane unless acted on by an external force. This fits within our idea of conservation of momentum, and thus we can now believe in the Kerr metric. Now we can move onto the magic that happens around a spinning black hole.

Properties of Spinning Black Holes

With the Schwarszchild metric, the first weirdness occurred when we set $r = 2M$. This made the $dr^2$ term blow up (aka $dr^2 \to \infty$). However, look again at the coefficient of the $dr^2$ term in the Kerr Metric. $$- \frac 1 {1 - \frac {2M} r + \frac {a^2} {r^2}}$$ Now, even if $r = 2M$, the coefficient will be $$- \frac 1 {1 - 1 + \frac {a^2} {4M^2}}$$ which means we will not be dividing by zero. This is a semi-problem, but a cool one nonetheless.

The event horizon is a cool name for the limit $r = 2M$. But now, that doesn't hold much significance as the $dr^2$ term doesn't approach $\infty$. The new event horizon is defined by: $$r_h = M \pm \sqrt{(M^2 - a^2)} $$ Let's run through this to so that we see what happens when we have various conditions of spin.

First, let's imagine we have a non-spinning black hole ($a = 0$). What happens when we plug this into our new horizon coordinate? $$r_h = M \pm \sqrt{(M^2 - (0)^2)} $$ $$r_h = M \pm \sqrt{M^2}$$ $$r_h = M \pm M$$ Well, now we have shown that there is a horizon at $r=2M$ for non-spinning black holes, which is exactly what we get from the Schwarzschild metric. You might have noticed that we have two values for the horizon now, since we have a $\pm$ in there. With non-spinning black holes, there is a horizon at $r = 2M$ and $r = 0$. That seems kind of weird, but will be better understood once we go through the next steps.

If you take any $a \gt 0$, you will find that both values of $r_h$ shrinks. It's simple to see that if $a \gt M$, then you get an imaginary number in the rooted term in $r_h$ ($M^2 - a^2 \lt 0$). Thus, the black hole cannot have angular momentum greater than $M$.

Two Horizons

Right now, you may be asking yourself: "What the heck does it mean to have two horizons? I mean, the event horizon is where space gets all wonky, but with spinning black holes, what happens when either value of the horizon is reached?" Let's plug in some values and check it out.

For example, let's take $\frac a M = \sqrt{\frac 3 4}$ (a personal favorite of Taylor). This makes $r_h = 1.5M$ and $.5M$. The event horizon is defined to be $1.5M$, and most of the behaviors attributed to the event horizon hold here. The horizon at $.5M$ is called the Cauchy horizon. For more information, go to this link. I'm not going to focus on this inner horizon at all, but it is a cool thought experiment.

We define a black hole to be EXTREME when $a = M$. From a few steps, we'll see that the event horizon and Cauchy horizon intersect around an extreme black hole (EBH). $$ r_h = M \pm \sqrt{(M^2 - M^2)} $$ $$ r_h = M $$ There's only one value for $r_h$ now; this means that the two horizons are at the same r-coordinate. We'll explore more about EBHs soon.

The New Metric

Extreme black holes are really cool. After all, they're EXTREME! Regardless, I want to plug in $a = M$ to the metric to see what happens. $$ d\tau^2 = (1 - \frac {2M} r)dt^2 + \frac {4Ma} r dtd\phi - \frac {dr^2} {1 - \frac {2M} r + \frac {a^2} {r^2}} - (1 + \frac {a^2} {r^2} + \frac {2Ma^2} {r^3})r^2d\phi $$ $$ d\tau^2 = (1 - \frac {2M} r)dt^2 + \frac {4M(M)} r dtd\phi - \frac {dr^2} {1 - \frac {2M} r + \frac {(M)^2} {r^2}} - (1 + \frac {(M)^2} {r^2} + \frac {2M(M)^2} {r^3})r^2d\phi $$ $$ d\tau^2 = (1 - \frac {2M} r)dt^2 + \frac {4M^2} r dtd\phi - \frac {dr^2} {(1 - \frac M r)^2} - (r^2 + M^2 + \frac {2M^3} {r})d\phi $$ We define $R^2 = r^2 + M^2 + \frac {2M^3} {r}$ so now: $$ d\tau^2 = (1 - \frac {2M} r)dt^2 + \frac {4M^2} r dtd\phi - \frac {dr^2} {(1 - \frac M r)^2} - R^2d\phi $$ This isn't exactly a new metric, but it does eliminate the pesky $a$ term and assumes extreme Kerr spacetime geometry (EKS). From now on I will use EBH and EKS unless denoted otherwise.

But now, briefly, pat yourself on the back if you said: "Wait, the limit $r = 2M$ is still important in the Kerr metric! Instead of the $dr^2$ term blowing up, the $dt^2$ term goes to $0$! What does that even mean?" You're not alone. It took me a while to understand the significance. What we're talking about is in the first term of the Kerr metric, namely: $$ (1- \frac {2M} r)dt^2 $$ When $r = 2M$, that coefficient turns into a big fat $0$. This beautiful thing is called the static limit! It is very important to the weirdness that happens around spinning black holes. Inside the static limit, nothing can resist rotation around the black hole, not even light!

The Static Limit

The static limit and event horizon intersect for a non-spinning black hole ($a = 0$). This is because the $dt^2$ coefficient remains unchanged as we change from the Schwarzschild metric to the Kerr metric. The region given by $M < r < 2M$ is called the ergosphere. This region between the static limit and the event horizon is noticeably weird once you wade into the math.

First, let's discuss the tangential motion of light. This means that the light is traveling with no change in radius ($dr^2 = 0$) and because it is light, our spacetime interval also is zero ($d\tau^2 = 0$). The EKS metric becomes: $$ 0 = (1 - \frac {2M} r)dt^2 + \frac {4M^2} r dtd\phi - R^2d\phi $$ Before going any further, we need to understand what we're solving for.

Our aim is to find $\frac {d\phi} {dt}$, which can be succintly explained as the change in azimuthal angle on the equatorial plane per tick on a clock at $r = \infty$. For those of you who are unfamiliar with Taylor's terms, he calls this $t$ interval the "bookkeeper". The bookkeeper is merely an observer outside of the effects of spacetime curvature around the black hole he/she is observing. So this interval $\frac {d\phi} {dt}$ is just the change in azimuthal angle light has, as observed by an observer extremely far away. Now to solve for it. Starting from the equation above... $$ 0 = (1 - \frac {2M} r) + \frac {4M^2} r \frac {d\phi} {dt} - R^2(\frac {d\phi} {dt})^2 $$ Using the quadratic formula with $a = -R^2$, $b = \frac {4M^2} r$, and $c = (1 - \frac {2M} r)$, we get: $$ \frac {d\phi} {dt} = \frac {{-4M^2 \over r} \pm \sqrt{(\frac {4M^2} r)^2 - 4(-R^2)(1 - \frac {2M} r)}} {-2R^2} $$ $$ \frac {d\phi} {dt} = {2M^2 \over rR^2} \pm \sqrt{({16M^4 \over r^2} + {1 \over R^2}(1 - {2M \over r}))({1 \over 4R^4})}$$ $$ {d\phi \over dt} = {2M^2 \over rR^2} \pm \sqrt{ {4M^4 \over r^2R^4} + {1 \over R^2}(1-{2M \over r})} $$ $$ {d\phi \over dt} = {2M^2 \over rR^2} \pm \sqrt{ {4M^4 \over r^2R^4} + ({4M^4 \over r^2R^4})({r^2R^2 \over 4M^4})(1-{2M \over r})} $$ $$ {d\phi \over dt} = {2M^2 \over rR^2} \pm {2M^2 \over rR^2}\sqrt{1 + ({r^2R^2 \over 4M^4})(1-{2M \over r})} $$

Alright, now we have a proper equation for ${d\phi \over dt}$. At the static limit, ${d\phi \over dt} = 0$ and ${1 \over 3M}$. What does this mean? First, the two values represent different initial directions for light. If you stood at the static limit and flashed your flashlight tangentially in the direction of the black hole's rotation, the bookkeeper would see it move with ${d\phi \over dt} = {1 \over 3M}$. In other words, it would move smoothly with the spacetime current.

On the other hand, we see that if you were to shine your flashlight in the other direction... the bookkeeper would not see it change in its azimuthal angle around the black hole. The light would stand still. What does that even mean? This is one of the first signs that black holes can actually drag spacetime with them as they spin. The nice and easy geometry of the Schwarzschild metric is no more. Now we have twisty space around spinning black holes. Despite it being mildly more complicated, it's actually really interesting and fun. Even the Earth has this effect on spacetime. You can read more about it on this page which explains an experiment in which researchers at Stanford used gyroscopes in satellites to determine how twisty space became in orbit around Earth.
